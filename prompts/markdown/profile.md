# Profile for Legal-RAG Prompt Engineering

**Based on Analysis of Three Supreme Court Criminal Cases:**
1. *Samia v. United States*, No. 22-196 (March 29, 2023) - Confrontation Clause/Bruton Rule
2. *Betterman v. Montana*, No. 14-1457 (March 28, 2016) - Speedy Trial Clause
3. *Glossip v. Oklahoma*, No. 22-7466 (October 9, 2024) - Due Process/Brady Violations

---

## I. Defense Attorney Argument Structure

### A. Opening Strategy

**1. Lead with Core Legal Principle**
- Defense attorneys immediately establish the controlling legal standard
- *Example (Samia)*: "Over 50 years ago, in Bruton versus United States, this Court held that the admission of a nontestifying defendant's confession that accuses another defendant in a joint trial violates the Confrontation Clause..."
- *Example (Glossip)*: "Richard Glossip was convicted on the word of one man, Justin Sneed, the undisputed murderer in this case. Oklahoma has now disclosed evidence revealing that Mr. Sneed lied to the jury..."

**Key Characteristics:**
- Cite foundational case law immediately (within first 2-3 sentences)
- Frame the issue as a clear constitutional violation
- Use concrete facts that resonate emotionally while maintaining legal precision

**2. State Remedy Sought Explicitly**
- Clear, unambiguous request for relief
- *Example (Samia)*: "If Bruton is to mean anything, Petitioner is entitled to a new trial without the introduction of the unconfronted confession."
- *Example (Glossip)*: "This Court should reverse and remand for a new trial."

### B. Handling Judicial Questions

**1. Confrontational/Skeptical Questions**
Defense attorneys employ several strategies:

**Strategy 1: Acknowledge then Redirect**
- When Justice Thomas asks about inference being testimonial (Samia), attorney acknowledges the point but redirects: "As in Gray, the confession here is directly accusatory. It's directly accusatory of someone."

**Strategy 2: Distinguish Unfavorable Precedent**
- When faced with Justice Gorsuch's comparison to Miranda exceptions: "What makes this different from run-of-the-mill evidentiary contexts, where you're talking about admitting evidence for one purpose but not another, is that the government has alternative options."

**Strategy 3: Concede Minor Points to Preserve Major Argument**
- *Example (Betterman)*: When asked about remedy difference, attorney concedes: "Your Honor, at the guilt stage of the prosecution, the outcomes are binary...At sentencing, the situation is quite different."

**2. Multi-Question Barrages**
When facing rapid-fire questioning (common with Justices Alito, Sotomayor):

**Technique:**
- Answer the immediate question briefly
- Attempt to pivot back to core argument
- If interrupted, accept the interruption gracefully
- Return to prepared talking points when possible

*Example (Glossip)*: When Justice Sotomayor asks three complex questions in succession, Waxman responds: "Okay" (acknowledging), then provides structured responses numbered "number one... number two..."

### C. Argumentation Techniques

**1. Factual Context Weaving**
Defense attorneys integrate facts throughout legal argument:
- Not just in opening, but as supporting examples for each legal point
- *Example (Samia)*: After stating Bruton rule, immediately provides: "the prosecution used the confession functionally to identify Petitioner. The prosecution's questioning of the agent who took the confession left little doubt..."

**2. Slippery Slope/Parade of Horribles**
- Warn about consequences of adverse ruling
- *Example (Samia)*: "The government's alternative approach would draw arbitrary and formalistic distinctions and permit ready circumvention of the Bruton rule, as this case illustrates."

**3. Analogical Reasoning**
- Compare to established precedent
- Distinguish when necessary
- *Example*: Multiple references to how lower courts have handled similar situations successfully

**4. Policy Arguments (Used Sparingly)**
Defense attorneys generally avoid pure policy:
- Focus remains on constitutional text and precedent
- Policy only when tightly linked to constitutional values
- *Example (Betterman)*: "The defendant's defense may be impaired by a delay in criminal proceedings, may be more significant at the sentencing stage because it may be the only place where the defendant challenges an upward adjustment or contests facts."

### D. Handling Weak Points

**1. Procedural Obstacles**
When facing procedural bars or waiver arguments:

**Strategy:** Address head-on but minimize
- *Example (Glossip)*: Glossip's attorney acknowledges procedural issues but argues: "legions of cases say that [waiver is always available under Oklahoma law], correct?"
- Then pivots: "So now we're on the federal issue, okay? That's all I'm looking at. Was there a Napue? Was there a Brady violation?"

**2. Unfavorable Facts**
Two approaches observed:

**Approach A: Reframe the Significance**
- *Example*: When challenged that lithium use was known to jury, defense reframes: "The issue wasn't about him taking lithium. The issue was about why he was taking the lithium."

**Approach B: Minimize and Move On**
- Don't dwell on weak points
- Provide minimal answer and return to strength

### E. Closing and Rebuttal

**Rebuttal Structure**:
1. **Address Most Damaging Points First**
   - Don't rehash everything
   - Target 2-3 specific misstatements or weak arguments from opposition

2. **Restate Core Holding Desired**
   - *Example (Samia)*: "Our fundamental ask for the Court is that the Court should hold...that the fundamental question is whether it is likely that the jury would infer that a redacted confession identified the nonconfessing defendant as an accomplice"

3. **End with Impact**
   - Return to human element when appropriate
   - Reinforce constitutional stakes

## II. Prosecution/Government Argument Structure

### A. Opening Strategy

**1. Procedural Defense First (When Available)**
Prosecutors often lead with threshold issues to avoid reaching merits:

- *Example (Samia - Government)*: Opens with defending existing precedent and limiting instructions
- *Example (Betterman - State)*: "The Speedy Trial Clause does not include sentencing delay because its purpose is to protect a presumptively innocent defendant from the harms associated with a criminal charge."

**2. Establish Limiting Principle**
Frame opponent's argument as extreme or unworkable:
- *Example (Samia)*: "The Bruton exception to that principle simply holds that when it comes to a discrete category of statements...we will no longer presume that the jury can obey that instruction."
- Emphasize narrowness of existing exceptions
- Warn against expansion

### B. Defensive Posture Management

**1. Defending Precedent**
When government supports existing conviction:

**Structure:**
- Acknowledge precedent exists
- Argue it's already at proper boundary
- Distinguish current case from precedent
- *Example (Samia)*: "This Court already approved of a confession like this in Gray, one that was modified there to refer to 'other guys' who also committed the crime"

**2. Burden-Shifting**
Consistently argue defendant hasn't met their burden:
- *Example (Betterman)*: "The defendant should have to show prejudice"
- *Example*: Reference to "speculative" harms repeatedly

**3. Practical Consequences**
Government attorneys heavily rely on practical impact arguments:
- *Example (Samia)*: "pretrial discovery proceedings, the risk of appellate reversals under a totality-of-the-circumstances standard, and the reality that, to avoid these problems, prosecutors will often be forced to forego confessions for joint trials"
- *Example (Betterman)*: Amicus brief from states warning this would be "a huge problem"

### C. Handling Questions

**1. Concessions (Rare but Strategic)**
When forced to concede a point:
- Minimize scope of concession
- Immediately follow with "but" statement
- *Example (Betterman - Montana)*: When asked about remedy for extreme delay: "I think if a defendant could show prejudice, for example, if he was not able to present mitigating evidence at sentencing because of passage of time, a lost witness, that may be one example."

**2. Hypotheticals**
Prosecutors handle hypotheticals differently than defense:

**Government Strategy:**
- Answer narrowly
- Avoid extending principles beyond immediate case
- *Example (Samia)*: When Justice Kagan poses "John and Mary" hypothetical, government attorney carefully works through each variation before committing to answer

**3. Distinguish Without Undermining**
Critical skill: distinguishing cases without suggesting broader principle:
- *Example*: Arguing case law doesn't apply here while not inviting Court to overrule precedent

### D. Alternative Grounds Strategy

**Prosecution Structure:**
1. Lead with strongest procedural ground
2. Add "even if" framing for merits
3. Argue merits in alternative
4. *Example (Samia)*: "First, confessions that replace a defendant's name with a natural-sounding noun or pronoun do not give rise to an overwhelming probability of juror disobedience...In fact, as has been discussed this morning already, this Court already approved of a confession like this in Gray"

### E. Amicus Support

Government often coordinates with or references:
- Other states
- Victims' families (Glossip)
- Law enforcement organizations

## III. Special Case: Confession of Error
(When State Switches Sides - *Glossip v. Oklahoma*)

### A. Unique Posture

In *Glossip*, Oklahoma's Attorney General confessed error and argued FOR the defendant, creating unusual dynamics:

**Attorney General (Clement) for State Supporting Petitioner:**

**1. Credibility Establishment**
- Emphasize thorough investigation: "after commissioning an independent review, he reluctantly reached the conclusion"
- Note continued prosecution of other cases: "he continues to defend multiple capital convictions"
- Stress obligation over preference: "Brady and Napue violations by the State's own prosecutors obligated him to confess error"

**2. Dual Audience**
Must persuade both:
- Court that original prosecutors violated Constitution
- Court that state supreme court erred in rejecting confession

**3. Handling Criticism of Prosecutors**
Careful balance:
- Acknowledge procedural irregularities without personal attacks
- Focus on systematic failures
- *Example*: "The attorney general had to deal what was in front of him and in the record. And particularly when you read the notes in light of that sheet, all of which is in the record now, I think General Drummond made the correct conclusion"

### B. Court-Appointed Amicus (Opposing Side)

**Role:** Defend judgment below when all parties agree it was wrong

**Strategy:**
- Challenge factual interpretations
- Emphasize alternative explanations
- Stress need for evidentiary hearing
- *Example*: Argue notes could have innocent explanation; that prosecutors weren't adequately interviewed

**Tone:**
- Respectful but skeptical
- Position as safeguarding judicial process
- Careful not to appear to defend actual misconduct

## IV. Structural Patterns Across Observed Attorneys

### A. Time Management

**Opening Statements:**
- 2-3 minutes uninterrupted (if lucky)
- Front-load most important points
- Expect interruption

**Question Periods:**
- Typically 20-30 minutes of questions
- Justice questions come in waves (each Justice gets turn)
- Some Justices ask multiple questions in rapid succession
- Others ask single, targeted questions

**Rebuttal:**
- Usually 3-5 minutes
- Highly selective

### B. Citation Practices

**1. Case Names**
- Full citation in opening or first reference
- Subsequent references by name only
- Foundational cases assumed known (like "Brady," "Crawford")

**2. Record References**
- Use when accuracy crucial: "page 960 of the Joint Appendix"
- Use when disputed: "this is reproduced in our brief at pages 9 to 11"
- Avoid over-citing (bogs down oral flow)

**3. Precedent Hierarchy**
Clear hierarchy in citation:
1. Supreme Court precedent (primary)
2. Circuit splits (to show issue importance)
3. Lower court consensus (to show workability)

### C. Rhetorical Devices

**1. Rhetorical Questions (Used Sparingly)**
- More common in written briefs
- In oral argument, risky (Justice may answer)
- *Example (Samia defense)*: "How could defense counsel have known that in Ms. Smothermon's files there was a memo..."

**2. Numbered Lists**
Effective for complex arguments:
- "Number one... Number two... Number three..."
- Helps Justices track multi-part answers
- *Example (Glossip)*: Waxman uses this extensively when responding to Justice Sotomayor

**3. Analogies (Carefully)**
- Must be legally precise
- Avoid being too clever
- *Example*: Comparing jury instruction reliability across contexts

**4. Concessions**
Strategic admission of weak points:
- Builds credibility
- Allows focus on strong points
- *Example*: "I don't deny that the entirety of paragraph 26 is a near-verbatim recitation of the two prongs of the state procedural bar."

### D. Argument "Beats"

Across all three cases, successful arguments follow similar beats:

1. **Opening Hook** (0-30 seconds)
   - State the core injustice or legal principle

2. **Legal Framework** (30 seconds - 2 minutes)
   - Establish controlling law
   - Cite key precedent
   - Frame standard

3. **Application** (2-4 minutes or until interrupted)
   - Facts meet legal standard
   - Distinguish opponent's interpretation

4. **Question Response** (15-25 minutes)
   - Answer directly when possible
   - Redirect when necessary
   - Never evade

5. **Closing** (if time permits)
   - Restate core holding sought
   - Emphasize stakes

## V. Distinguishing Features by Attorney Role

### Defense Attorney

**Strengths:**
- Moral urgency (liberty at stake)
- Constitutional tradition (favor the accused)
- Specific client focus

**Challenges:**
- Burden of proof (must show constitutional violation)
- Precedent (government usually defending existing rules)
- Practical concerns (judges worry about disruption)

**Tone:**
- Urgent but not desperate
- Respectful but persistent
- Grounded in precedent but forward-looking

### Prosecution/Government Attorneys

**Strengths:**
- Presumption of regularity
- Practical consequences argument
- Finality interests

**Challenges:**
- Harder to claim moral high ground
- Must defend specific conduct (not abstract principle)
- Collective responsibility (answering for other prosecutors)

**Tone:**
- Measured and procedural
- Emphasize stability and workability
- Minimize disruption

### Court-appointed Amicus

**Unique Characteristics:**
- No client to satisfy
- Purely legal mission
- Can take unpopular positions

**Tone:**
- Institutionally focused
- Process-protective
- Academically rigorous

## VI. Critical Skills for Legal RAG

### A. Question Handling

**What Makes a Good Answer:**
1. **Directness**
   - Answer the question asked
   - Don't evade unless question is trap

2. **Conciseness**
   - Justices can interrupt
   - Short answers allow follow-up

3. **Strategic Depth**
   - Know when to elaborate
   - Know when to stop

**What Makes a Bad Answer:**
1. Circular reasoning
2. Repetition without adding value
3. Arguing with Justice (rather than persuading)
4. Failing to answer at all

### B. Distinguishing Cases

**Effective Distinction:**
- Precise factual difference
- Legal significance of difference
- Not just "this case is different"

**Example (from Samia):**
"The factual difference here is that the redaction was not apparent on its face. Our test recognizes that. Our submission would be that the redaction here was pretty obvious by virtue of the way in which this confession came in."

### C. Handling Hypotheticals

**Pattern:**
1. Clarify hypothetical if ambiguous
2. Answer the hypothetical directly
3. Distinguish from actual case if necessary
4. Return to actual facts

**Example:**
Justice asks: "Suppose instead there's something that says, redacted and I went out and robbed Bill?"
Answer structure: First address hypothetical, then: "But it's neither of those two things. Instead, the confession says..."

### D. When to Fight, When to Concede

**Fight when:**
- Core legal principle at stake
- Factual dispute crucial to outcome
- Justice misunderstands your position

**Concede when:**
- Peripheral point
- Fighting hurts credibility
- Better to reframe than deny

## VII. Argument Comparison: Defense VS. Prosecution

### Opening Gambits

**Defense**
- Lead with constitutional violation
- Client-specific facts
- "This Court held..."
- Future-focused (remedy)

**Prosecution**
- Lead with procedural adequacy
- Categorical rules
- "This Court has been careful not to extend..."
- Past-focused (finality)

### During Questions

**Defense**
- Push for broad principle
- Emphasize individual justice
- Point to lower court success
- "This Court should hold..."

**Prosecution**
- Argue narrow application
- Emphasize systemic effects
- Point to lower court chaos
- "This Court need not decide..."

### Closing

**Defense**
- Restate requested holding
- Return to client
- Call for action

**Prosecution**
- Restate limiting principle
- Return to system
- Call for restraint

## VIII. Case-specific Insights (just in case)

### A. *Samia v. United States* (Confrontation clause)

**Issue:** Whether placeholder redactions ("the other person") in co-defendant's confession violate Bruton rule

**Defense Strategy:**
- Functional equivalence argument
- "Even if confession not facially obvious, it functionally identified petitioner"
- Emphasis on context: number of defendants, how confession used, linking evidence

**Government Strategy:**
- Bright-line argument
- "Only facially incriminating confessions trigger Bruton"
- Practical necessity: joint trials important

**Key Exchange Pattern:**
- Justices repeatedly ask: "Where do you draw the line?"
- Defense: "Where jury likely to infer identification"
- Government: "Only where obvious on face of confession"

### B. *Betterman v. Montana* (Speedy Trial)

**Issue:** Whether Speedy Trial Clause applies to sentencing delay

**Defense Strategy:**
- Textual interpretation: "trial" includes sentencing
- Practical impact: sentencing is where real adjudication happens now
- Barker factors can be tailored

**Government Strategy:**
- Historical practice: sentencing not part of "trial"
- Remedy problem: dismissal makes no sense for sentencing
- Due Process Clause provides adequate protection

**Key Exchange Pattern:**
- Justices fixated on remedy: "What would you do?"
- Defense: Reduce sentence proportionally
- Government: Would require showing actual prejudice

### C. *Glossip v. Oklahoma* (Brady/Napue)

**Issue:** Whether prosecution suppressed material evidence and failed to correct false testimony

**Unique Posture:**
- State confesses error
- Victim's family opposes
- Court appoints amicus to defend judgment

**AG (for Defendant) Strategy:**
- Credibility through reluctance
- Documentary evidence
- Independent investigations

**Amicus (Defending Judgment) Strategy:**
- Evidentiary hearing needed
- Prosecutors' current explanations
- Alternative interpretations

**Key Exchange Pattern:**
- Justices skeptical of all parties
- Procedural issues dominate
- Substantive Brady/Napue questions almost secondary

## IX. Summary

### Universal Principles

1. **Structure beats eloquence** - Clear organization more important than rhetorical flourishes
2. **Concede strategically** - Builds credibility for contested points
3. **Case law is currency** - Every argument must be grounded in precedent
4. **Facts matter differently** - Defense emphasizes specific facts; prosecution emphasizes categories
5. **Questions are opportunities** - Best arguments emerge from Justice questioning

### Role-Specific Priorities

**Defense:**
- Liberty
- Individual justice
- Constitutional text
- Forward-looking

**Prosecution:**
- Order
- Systemic function
- Practical limits
- Backward-looking (precedent/finality)

### Critical Skills

1. **Distinguishing precedent** (most common move)
2. **Answering hypotheticals** (tests principle's limits)
3. **Handling hostile questions** (without being defensive)
4. **Time management** (prioritizing under pressure)
5. **Strategic concessions** (knowing what to defend)

### Strats for RAG

**Query Strategy:**
- Search for analogous fact patterns
- Find cases distinguishing same precedent
- Locate circuit splits on issue
- Retrieve standards of review

**Prompt Strategy:**
- Include debate structure rules (turn-taking)
- Define each agent's win conditions
- Specify citation requirements
- Build in "hostile questioning" phase
- Allow agents to request case law retrieval
